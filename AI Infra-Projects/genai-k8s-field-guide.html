01

AI & Machine Learning Foundations

AI world Chapter 1

The nested family tree that GenAI sits inside. Get this hierarchy straight first — the whole book assumes you know that an LLM is a very specific kind of a much broader thing.

Artificial Intelligence

AI

Any technique that makes a machine do things we'd call "intelligent" — the broadest umbrella, from 1970s rule-engines to today's chatbots.

Machine Learning

ML

Software that learns patterns from examples rather than following rules a programmer wrote by hand — show it 10,000 spam emails and it figures out what spam looks like.

Deep Learning

DL

ML built from neural networks stacked many layers deep, loosely inspired by how brain neurons connect — this is what made modern AI actually work.

Generative AI

GenAI

Deep learning that produces new content — text, images, music, code — instead of just classifying or predicting. The whole subject of this book.

Neural Network

A web of simple math units ("neurons") in layers that pass signals forward; training nudges the connection strengths until the right outputs come out.

CNN

Convolutional NN

A neural net built for images — it scans for visual features (edges, shapes) much like your eye's visual cortex. Great at "what's in this picture?"

RNN

Recurrent NN

A neural net for sequences (text, time-series) that reads one item at a time and remembers what came before — the pre-transformer way to handle language.

LSTM / GRU

memory cells

Upgraded RNNs with a built-in "memory gate" so they don't forget the start of a long sentence by the time they reach the end.

NLP

Natural Language Processing

The field of getting computers to understand and produce human language — the home turf of LLMs.

SVM

Support Vector Machine

A classic (pre-deep-learning) ML method that draws the cleanest possible dividing line between two categories of data.

Backpropagation

+ gradient descent

The core training trick: measure how wrong the network was, then walk the error backwards through every layer to tweak each knob slightly in the right direction. Repeat millions of times.

SOTA

State Of The Art

The current best-performing model or technique for a task — the bar everyone benchmarks against.

02

Transformers & Large Language Models

AI world Chapter 1

The 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" architecture that powers every modern LLM. You don't need the math, but you do need the vocabulary — tokens, embeddings, attention — because it explains why these models are so hungry for GPU memory.

Transformer

The neural-network design (2017) behind all modern LLMs. Its superpower is processing a whole sentence in parallel while still tracking how every word relates to every other.

Foundation Model

FM

A huge model pre-trained on broad data that serves as a reusable base — you adapt it to your task rather than training from scratch. Think "base image" for AI.

Mentally map it to a golden AMI or base container image: expensive to build once, then everyone customizes on top.

Large Language Model

LLM

A foundation model specialised for language — it predicts the next word over and over to generate fluent text. GPT, Claude, and Llama are LLMs.

Token / Tokenization

Models don't read words — they read tokens, the sub-word chunks text is chopped into. Pricing, context limits, and speed are all measured in tokens.

Embedding

vector

A list of numbers that captures a token's meaning as coordinates in space, so "king" and "queen" land near each other. The universal currency of GenAI.

Self-Attention

multi-head

The mechanism that lets each word "look at" all the others and decide which matter most for meaning — like a team of detectives each chasing a different clue in the same sentence.

Query / Key / Value

Q · K · V

The three vectors attention computes per word: the question a word is asking, the labels other words offer, and the information retrieved when they match.

Positional Encoding

Since attention sees all words at once, this adds a "position stamp" so the model knows word order — that "dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog".

Encoder / Decoder

The two halves of a transformer: the encoder understands the input, the decoder generates the output. Chat models are mostly decoder-only.

Context Window

The maximum number of tokens a model can consider at once — its short-term memory. Bigger windows fit more of your documents but cost more GPU memory.

Parameters / Weights

The billions of internal numbers a model learns during training. "A 70B model" means 70 billion of them — and roughly how much GPU RAM you'll need to hold it.

Inference

Actually using a trained model to get an answer (as opposed to training it). In production this is the workload you scale, monitor, and pay for per request.

This is your bread and butter: inference is just a stateless-ish service you deploy, autoscale, and observe — only it's pinned to a GPU.

Training

The expensive up-front process of teaching a model from data by adjusting all its weights — done once (or rarely), often across many GPUs for days.

GPT

Generative Pre-trained Transformer

OpenAI's family of decoder-only LLMs that popularised the "chatbot" era; the archetype most people picture when they hear "LLM".

BERT

Bidirectional Encoder

Google's encoder-style transformer that reads text both directions at once — excellent at understanding (search, classification) rather than generating.

Temperature

A dial (0–1+) controlling randomness: low = safe, repeatable answers; high = creative, varied ones. You tune it per use case.

Hallucination

When a model states something false with total confidence. The main reason production systems bolt on RAG to keep answers grounded in real data.

Vector Database

Milvus · Pinecone · FAISS

A database that stores embeddings and finds the most similar ones fast — the retrieval engine behind RAG. It answers "what's closest in meaning?" not "what matches exactly?"

Cosine Similarity

/ Euclidean distance

The math for measuring how "close" two embeddings are — i.e. how related two pieces of text are in meaning. It's what a vector search actually computes.

03

Containers & Kubernetes Core

Infra world Chapters 2–3

This is your living room — you know it cold. Skim it. The only new angle: the book frames every K8s primitive around packaging a model + its heavy dependencies and scheduling it onto GPU nodes, so the definitions below flag where GenAI changes the calculus.

Container

A lightweight, isolated package holding an app plus everything it needs to run, sharing the host OS kernel. You know this — for GenAI it also bundles the model weights and CUDA libs.

Container Image

/ Dockerfile

The read-only blueprint a container is launched from. GenAI images are large (multi-GB model + framework), which is why registry, caching, and pull-time suddenly matter.

Docker

The tooling that builds and runs containers from a Dockerfile — the on-ramp the book uses to package your first GenAI model locally.

Registry

e.g. ECR

A store for container images that clusters pull from. Amazon ECR is the AWS one used throughout the book.

OCI

Open Container Initiative

The vendor-neutral standard for image and runtime formats, so an image built anywhere runs anywhere.

Kubernetes

K8s

The orchestrator that schedules, scales, heals, and networks containers across a fleet of machines. The book's whole premise: it's the ideal control layer for GPU-hungry GenAI.

Pod

The smallest deployable unit — one or more containers that live and die together. A model-serving pod is what you'll scale and pin to a GPU.

Node

A worker machine (an EC2 instance) that runs pods. For GenAI these are often pricey GPU instances — which flips your cost and scheduling instincts.

Your CPU-node autoscaling intuition mostly holds, but a GPU node can cost 20–40× a CPU node, so "just add nodes" is now a budget decision.

Cluster

The control plane plus all its worker nodes, managed as one system. Usually a mix of cheap CPU nodes and a smaller pool of GPU nodes.

Control Plane

The "brain" — API server, scheduler, etcd, controllers — that stores desired state and continuously drives reality toward it. On EKS, AWS runs this for you.

kubelet

The agent on each node that talks to the control plane and makes sure the pods it's told to run are actually running and healthy.

CRI

Container Runtime Interface

The pluggable API letting kubelet drive whatever runtime (containerd, CRI-O) actually starts containers.

kubectl

The command-line tool you use to talk to a cluster — apply YAML, inspect pods, read logs.

YAML / Manifest

The declarative text format where you describe what you want (deployments, services, GPU requests); Kubernetes makes it real.

Deployment

A controller that keeps N identical pod replicas running and handles rolling updates — how you ship and version a model service.

Service

A stable network endpoint + load-balancer in front of a set of pods, so callers don't care which replica answers.

Namespace

A logical partition inside a cluster for isolating teams, environments, or tenants — familiar from your multi-tenant platform work.

Helm

The "package manager" for Kubernetes — bundles many manifests into one templated, versioned install. Most GenAI tools (Prometheus, GPU Operator) ship as Helm charts.

CRD

Custom Resource Definition

A way to teach Kubernetes a brand-new object type. GPU, autoscaling, and MLOps tools all register CRDs to extend the API.

Operator

Software that packages an app's operational know-how as a controller watching CRDs. The NVIDIA GPU Operator is the one you'll meet — it installs drivers, device plugins, and DCGM for you.

An Operator is to a GPU driver stack what a Deployment controller is to replicas: it watches desired state and reconciles the messy setup automatically.

kind

K8s in Docker

A tiny throwaway cluster that runs inside Docker on your laptop — the book uses it for local testing before you touch AWS.

CNCF

Cloud Native Computing Foundation

The open-source home of Kubernetes and most cloud-native tooling — a signal of which projects are "safe" bets.

04

Cloud & AWS Building Blocks

Infra world Chapter 3

All familiar. The book standardises on Amazon EKS provisioned with Terraform, and adds two AWS-specific accelerators (Trainium/Inferentia) as a cheaper alternative to NVIDIA GPUs — worth knowing for cost conversations.

AWS

Amazon Web Services

The cloud the book builds on. You live here already.

Amazon EKS

Elastic K8s Service

AWS's managed Kubernetes — AWS runs the control plane, you bring the (GPU) worker nodes. The book's default platform, and your day job.

EC2

Elastic Compute Cloud

AWS virtual machines — your K8s nodes are EC2 instances. GenAI adds GPU families (P/G series) to the menu.

EBS

Elastic Block Store

Network-attached disks for EC2/pods — where model weights and datasets often live. The book's storage-cost chapter tunes these (gp2 → gp3).

ECR

Elastic Container Registry

AWS's private image registry that EKS pulls your (large) GenAI images from.

VPC

Virtual Private Cloud

Your isolated network in AWS where the cluster runs — subnets, routing, security groups. Standard stuff for you.

Availability Zone

AZ

An isolated datacenter within a region. Spreading nodes across AZs is the base layer of the HA chapter — but GPU capacity per AZ can be scarce.

IAM

Identity & Access Management

AWS's permissions system — who/what can do what. The security chapter leans on it heavily.

IRSA

IAM Roles for Service Accounts

The clean way to give a specific pod specific AWS permissions (via OIDC) instead of sharing node-wide credentials.

OIDC

OpenID Connect

The identity-federation standard that lets EKS trade a pod's service-account token for real IAM permissions — the mechanism under IRSA.

Terraform / IaC

Declarative infrastructure provisioning. The book spins up the entire EKS cluster with Terraform — right in your wheelhouse.

eksctl

A CLI shortcut for creating and managing EKS clusters and node groups, used alongside Terraform in the book.

Trainium / Inferentia

+ Neuron SDK

AWS's own AI chips — Trainium for training, Inferentia for inference — a cheaper, NVIDIA-free option. The Neuron SDK is how your code targets them.

Your lever in cost reviews: if a workload fits, moving inference from GPU to Inferentia can cut the per-request bill substantially.

GKE / AKS / GCP

Google's and Azure's managed Kubernetes (and Google's cloud). Mentioned for portability — the patterns transfer across all three.

05

Model Customization — RAG, Fine-tuning & Friends

Bridge Chapters 4–5

The heart of the book, and the single most valuable concept for AI-infra work: a base model rarely knows your data, so you adapt it. There's a ladder of techniques from cheap-and-instant to expensive-and-deep. Know when to reach for which.

RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Fetch relevant snippets from your own data and feed them into the prompt so the model answers from facts instead of guessing. Kills hallucination, needs no retraining — and it's mostly infra to run.

Fine-tuning

Continue training a base model a little more on your specific data so it internalises your style, format, or domain. Requires GPUs and a curated dataset.

Prompt Engineering

Getting better outputs purely by crafting the input — instructions, examples, formatting. Zero infrastructure; always the first thing to try.

PEFT

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning

Fine-tuning that updates only a tiny slice of the model's weights instead of all of them — slashing GPU and memory cost. LoRA is the popular flavour.

LoRA

Low-Rank Adaptation

The most common PEFT method: freeze the big model, train small "adapter" matrices alongside it (80%+ fewer weights to update). Cheap, fast, swappable.

QLoRA

Quantized LoRA

LoRA plus quantization — compress the frozen model to 4-bit first, so you can fine-tune a big model on a single modest GPU.

Quantization

Shrinking a model by storing its numbers at lower precision (32-bit → 8-bit → 4-bit). Smaller, faster, cheaper to serve — with a small accuracy trade-off.

Directly a cost/latency lever you own: a quantized model may fit on a smaller GPU or serve more requests per node.

Distillation

Train a small "student" model to mimic a big "teacher" model — most of the quality at a fraction of the serving cost.

Pruning

Snip away the least-useful connections in a trained model to make it leaner and faster without much accuracy loss.

RLHF

RL from Human Feedback

Aligning a model to human preferences by having people rank its answers and training a reward signal from those rankings — how raw LLMs become polite assistants.

DPO

Direct Preference Optimization

A simpler, cheaper alternative to RLHF that tunes on preference pairs directly, skipping the separate reward model.

Reinforcement Learning

RL

Training by trial-and-error toward a reward signal rather than from labelled examples — the machinery underneath RLHF.

BLEU / ROUGE

Standard scores for grading generated text against reference answers — BLEU leans to translation, ROUGE to summarisation. Your "test pass rate" for model quality.

Model Selection

Choosing which base model to start from, trading off quality, size, license, and serving cost against your use case — an infra-flavoured decision.

Domain-Specific Optimization

The umbrella goal of Chapter 4: bending a general model toward your narrow domain (e-commerce, legal, medical) using the techniques above.

06

The GenAI Toolchain — Frameworks & Libraries

AI world Chapters 4–5

The concrete software you'll containerize and run. Think of these as the "npm/pip ecosystem" of GenAI — you don't need to be a data scientist, but you must know what each one is so you can package, deploy, and debug it.

LangChain

A framework that wires LLMs together with data sources, tools, and vector DBs — the "glue" most RAG apps and chatbots are built with.

Hugging Face

+ Transformers lib

The GitHub-of-models: where you download open models (like Llama 3) and the Python library to load and run them. You'll need an account for the book.

PyTorch

The dominant deep-learning framework models are built and trained in. You won't write it much, but your containers will ship it and it'll want the GPU.

TensorFlow

Google's older deep-learning framework — same role as PyTorch, still common in production estates.

JAX

A newer high-performance ML framework favoured for large-scale training; good to recognise, rarely your concern operationally.

Llama 3

Meta's popular open-weight LLM — the concrete model the book fine-tunes and deploys, because you can self-host it on your own EKS GPUs.

JupyterHub / Notebook

Browser-based interactive coding environments where data scientists experiment. In the book you deploy JupyterHub on K8s so a team shares GPU-backed notebooks.

A very common first "GenAI platform" ask you'll get: multi-user JupyterHub on the cluster with GPU scheduling — pure platform engineering.

SHAP

A technique for explaining why a model made a prediction by attributing it to input features — used in the bias/fairness discussion.

PCA

Principal Component Analysis

A classic method to squeeze high-dimensional data down to its most informative directions — handy for visualising or compressing embeddings.

EDA

Exploratory Data Analysis

The first-look phase of any ML project: profiling and visualising data before modelling.

07

Scaling & Autoscaling

Infra world Chapter 6

You know these autoscalers. The GenAI twist is that the thing you're scaling sits on scarce GPUs, so the choice of which autoscaler — and what metric triggers it — has real money attached.

HPA

Horizontal Pod Autoscaler

Adds or removes pod replicas based on load (CPU, or a custom metric like GPU use / requests-per-second). Scaling out your inference service.

VPA

Vertical Pod Autoscaler

Right-sizes the CPU/memory requests of each pod instead of adding more — useful for getting a model's resource asks correct.

KEDA

Event-Driven Autoscaling

Scales pods on any external signal — SQS queue length, Kafka lag, a schedule — not just CPU. Perfect for batch inference: spin up when the queue fills, down to zero when empty.

Cluster Autoscaler

CA

Adds/removes nodes (via Auto Scaling Groups) when pods can't fit or sit idle. The classic node-level scaler.

Karpenter

A smarter node autoscaler that provisions exactly the right instance type on demand — including the specific GPU a pending pod requested — often faster and cheaper than CA.

You've already evaluated Cast.AI vs Karpenter at Harman — same muscle, now the workloads it schedules are GPU model servers.

Custom vs Conventional Metrics

Conventional = CPU/memory. Custom = anything you export (tokens/sec, GPU utilization, queue depth) — GenAI almost always needs custom metrics to scale sensibly.

ASG

Auto Scaling Group

The AWS primitive that grows/shrinks a pool of identical EC2 instances — what Cluster Autoscaler drives under the hood.

08

Cost Optimization

Infra world Chapter 7

GPUs make cost the conversation. Your FinOps and right-sizing instincts transfer directly — this is one of the fastest ways to prove value as an AI-infra engineer, because a single idle GPU node burns real money.

Kubecost

A tool that breaks down Kubernetes spend by namespace/team/workload and flags savings — your chargeback and cost-visibility engine for GPU usage.

Goldilocks

Recommends "just right" CPU/memory requests for your pods (by watching VPA), so you stop over-provisioning.

Right-sizing

Matching requested resources to what's actually used — the single biggest cost lever, and doubly so when the resource is a GPU.

Spot Instances

Deeply discounted spare EC2 capacity that AWS can reclaim anytime — great for interruptible training/batch jobs, risky for always-on serving.

Savings Plans / Reserved

Commit to steady usage for a big discount — the play for baseline, always-on GPU inference capacity.

Cost Components

The three buckets the chapter optimises: compute (GPU-dominated), storage (model weights, datasets), and networking (data transfer). Compute usually dwarfs the rest.

09

Networking

Infra world Chapter 8

Your VPC-endpoint-and-CNI debugging past pays off here. The extra GenAI concern is bandwidth and latency: multi-GPU training shuffles huge tensors between nodes, so the network can become the bottleneck (hence EFA/RDMA in the GPU section).

CNI

Container Network Interface

The plugin standard that gives every pod its IP and connectivity (AWS VPC CNI on EKS). You know this cold.

kube-proxy · IPTables vs IPVS

The component routing Service traffic to pods; IPVS scales to far more services with lower overhead than the older IPTables mode.

Service Mesh

A sidecar layer (Istio/Linkerd) adding traffic control, mTLS, and telemetry between services without app changes — useful once you have several model + RAG microservices talking.

Network Policy

Firewall rules for pod-to-pod traffic — the book uses them to lock down which components of the chatbot can reach the model and the vector DB.

eBPF

A way to run sandboxed programs inside the Linux kernel — powers modern high-performance networking, security, and observability (e.g. Cilium) with minimal overhead.

SR-IOV

Single-Root I/O Virtualization

Lets a physical network card present itself as many virtual ones straight to pods — near-bare-metal network speed for demanding GPU workloads.

CoreDNS

The cluster's internal DNS — how pods find services by name. Familiar territory.

NLB / ALB / Ingress

The load balancers (Network/Application) and routing layer exposing your model endpoints to the outside world — the AWS Load Balancer Controller you already know.

Overlay vs Native · CIDR

Two ways to give pods addresses (a virtual overlay network vs real VPC IPs), and the IP-range notation you carve subnets with.

TLS / HTTPS / mTLS

Encryption in transit; mTLS additionally verifies both ends — important when model endpoints handle sensitive prompts.

10

Security

Infra world Chapter 9

Defense-in-depth, exactly as you practise it. GenAI adds two fresh worries the chapter calls out: data privacy (prompts and training data may contain PII) and securing the model endpoint itself against abuse.

Defense in Depth

Layering many independent controls so no single failure is fatal — the organising principle of the whole chapter.

Supply Chain Security

Trusting what goes into your images — scanning base images, models, and dependencies. Bigger surface for GenAI because you're pulling third-party model weights.

RBAC

Role-Based Access Control

Who can do what inside the cluster — least-privilege for humans and workloads. Standard for you.

Secrets Management · KMS

Safely storing API keys, model-registry tokens, and DB creds — backed by AWS KMS for encryption. GenAI apps hold a lot of these.

OPA / Gatekeeper

Open Policy Agent

A policy engine that enforces guardrails at admission time ("no pod without resource limits", "only signed images"). You've deployed this pattern.

Kyverno

A Kubernetes-native policy engine (YAML, no new language) doing the same job as OPA — validate, mutate, and generate resources by policy.

PSS

Pod Security Standards

Built-in baseline/restricted profiles that stop pods running as root, escalating privileges, etc.

Data Privacy & Compliance

PII · HIPAA · PCI · SOC

Handling personal data lawfully — a first-class GenAI concern because prompts, logs, and training sets can leak PII. Governs what you may log and store.

This is where your platform-governance experience becomes a differentiator: someone has to make model endpoints audit-ready.

Secure Model Endpoints

Protecting the inference API itself — authN/Z, rate limits, input validation — against abuse, prompt injection, and cost-draining attacks.

WAF

Web Application Firewall

Filters malicious HTTP traffic before it reaches your endpoints — a front-line guard for public model APIs.

SIEM

Centralised security-event monitoring/alerting — where cluster and app security signals get correlated.

IMDS

Instance Metadata Service

The EC2 endpoint pods can reach for instance credentials — a known attack path you lock down (IMDSv2, hop limits).

11

GPUs & Accelerators

Bridge Chapter 10

This chapter is arguably your highest-leverage specialism — very few DevOps engineers understand GPU scheduling deeply. Master how Kubernetes exposes, partitions, and shares GPUs and you become genuinely hard to replace.

GPU

Graphics Processing Unit

A chip with thousands of small cores that crunch the massively parallel math LLMs need. The scarce, expensive resource that everything in this book optimises around.

CPU

The general-purpose processor — fine for app logic and small models, too slow for large-model math. GenAI clusters mix a few GPUs with many CPUs.

Accelerator

Any specialised AI chip (GPU, TPU, Trainium, Inferentia). "Accelerator-aware scheduling" is the skill of placing work on the right one.

CUDA

NVIDIA's programming layer that lets software use the GPU. You won't write CUDA, but version mismatches between it, the driver, and PyTorch are a classic thing you'll debug.

The "dependency hell" of GenAI ops: CUDA ↔ driver ↔ framework versions must line up. Owning that compatibility matrix is real, valued work.

Tensor Core

Specialised units inside modern NVIDIA GPUs that do the matrix math of deep learning especially fast — the reason newer GPUs crush older ones.

MIG

Multi-Instance GPU

Slice one physical A100/H100 into up to 7 fully isolated mini-GPUs, each with its own memory and cores — so several workloads share hardware without interfering.

MPS

Multi-Process Service

Lets multiple processes run on one GPU at the same time, sharing compute while keeping separate memory — fills idle capacity, but with weaker isolation than MIG.

GPU Time-Slicing

Workloads take turns on the whole GPU in rapid rotation — the simplest sharing, works on any GPU, but no isolation. Good for dev/notebook oversubscription.

DCGM

Data Center GPU Manager

NVIDIA's tool that exports GPU health and utilization metrics — your Prometheus target for "are we actually using these expensive GPUs?"

GPU Operator · Device Plugin

The Operator auto-installs drivers, the device plugin advertises GPUs to the scheduler (as nvidia.com/gpu), and manages MIG. This is how K8s even "sees" GPUs.

NVIDIA NIM

Inference Microservice

Pre-packaged, optimised model-serving containers from NVIDIA — deploy a performant inference endpoint without hand-tuning the serving stack.

EFA

Elastic Fabric Adapter

AWS's high-speed, low-latency network interface for tightly-coupled GPU nodes — what multi-GPU distributed training needs so the network isn't the bottleneck.

RDMA

Remote Direct Memory Access

Lets one node read another node's memory directly, bypassing the CPU — the tech (under EFA) that makes cross-node GPU training fast.

NCCL

NVIDIA's library for shuffling data between many GPUs efficiently during distributed training — the collective-communication layer on top of RDMA.

GPU Utilization

How busy a GPU actually is. Low utilization = money burning; the whole chapter exists to push this number up via sharing and right-sizing.

Operating GenAI Workloads on K8s

12

GenAIOps & the Automation Pipeline

Bridge Chapter 11

MLOps for GenAI: the CI/CD-style plumbing that moves a model from experiment to production and keeps it healthy. This is literally your DevOps discipline applied to models — the most natural extension of your current role.

GenAIOps / MLOps

The practices and tooling to build, deploy, monitor, and continuously improve models reliably — DevOps principles, model-shaped. Your core transferable skill.

Kubeflow

A Kubernetes-native platform for end-to-end ML workflows — pipelines, training, serving — all as K8s resources you can operate.

MLflow

Tracks experiments, model versions, and a model registry — the "source control + artifact store" for models. Answers "which model version is in prod and how was it trained?"

Ray / KubeRay

A framework for distributing Python/ML work across a cluster; KubeRay runs Ray clusters on Kubernetes — how you scale training and batch inference across many nodes.

Argo Workflows

A Kubernetes-native engine for running multi-step pipelines as DAGs — data prep → fine-tune → evaluate → deploy, orchestrated on the cluster. You know Argo CD; this is its batch-pipeline sibling.

GenAI Pipeline

The automated chain from raw data to a deployed, monitored model — the thing GenAIOps tooling builds and runs.

Model Drift

When a model's quality quietly degrades because the real world changed but the model didn't — you monitor for it and trigger retraining. Like alerting on SLO erosion.

Model Bias

Systematic unfairness in outputs (skewed by training data). Detecting and mitigating it is part of responsible deployment.

Bias & Variance

The classic trade-off: too simple a model underfits (bias), too complex overfits (variance). Tested to make sure a model generalises.

13

Observability

Bridge Chapter 12

You already run Prometheus, Grafana, and the ELK/Loki stack — this chapter is home. The only additions are two GPU/LLM-specific layers: DCGM for GPU metrics, and LangFuse/LangChain tracing for what happens inside a model call.

Observability

Being able to understand what a system is doing from its outputs — the umbrella over logs, metrics, and traces. Your existing craft.

Logs · Metrics · Traces

The three signal types: text events, numeric time-series, and end-to-end request paths. Together they answer what / how much / where.

Prometheus

The de-facto metrics collector and time-series DB in Kubernetes, queried with PromQL. You run this daily.

Grafana

The dashboards-and-alerts front-end over your metrics and logs — the "single pane of glass". Firmly in your comfort zone.

Loki

Grafana's log store — "Prometheus, but for logs" — cheap and label-indexed. A lighter alternative to the ELK stack you know.

Fluentd / Fluent Bit

Log shippers that collect container logs and forward them to a backend (Loki, Elasticsearch). Fluent Bit is the lightweight edition.

OpenTelemetry

OTel · ADOT

The vendor-neutral standard for emitting traces/metrics/logs; ADOT is AWS's distribution of it. Instrument once, send anywhere.

LangFuse

Observability built specifically for LLM apps — traces each prompt, response, token count, latency, and cost. The piece your generic stack doesn't give you.

LangChain Observability

Tracing the multi-step chains inside a LangChain app (retrieve → prompt → call → parse) so you can debug where a bad answer came from.

PromQL

Prometheus's query language for slicing metrics and writing alert rules — you've already authored these for disk/CPU/memory.

14

High Availability & Disaster Recovery

Infra world Chapter 13

Pure SRE — RPO, RTO, redundancy, multi-region. All directly transferable. The one wrinkle: GPU capacity is regionally scarce, so a GPU-workload DR plan has to account for whether you can even get GPUs in the failover region.

HA

High Availability

Designing so the service survives component failures with minimal downtime — redundancy, health checks, spreading across AZs. Your bread and butter.

DR

Disaster Recovery

The plan to restore service after a major outage — from backup-restore up to hot multi-region. Same discipline you practise now.

RPO

Recovery Point Objective

How much data you can afford to lose, in time ("up to 5 min"). Drives backup frequency for vector DBs, model registries, and datasets.

RTO

Recovery Time Objective

How fast you must be back up after failure. Tighter RTO = more standby (GPU) capacity = more cost.

MTD

Maximum Tolerable Downtime

The absolute ceiling of outage the business can survive — the outer bound your RTO must sit inside.

Redundancy

Running spare copies (pods, nodes, AZs, regions) so one failure isn't fatal — the foundation of both HA and DR.

Multi-Region

Running across geographically separate regions for the strongest resilience — with the GenAI caveat that GPU availability and data-residency vary by region.

Backup & Restore

The simplest DR tier: periodically snapshot state, restore on failure. Cheapest, but slowest RTO.

Resiliency

The system's overall ability to absorb failures and keep serving — the goal the whole chapter builds toward.
